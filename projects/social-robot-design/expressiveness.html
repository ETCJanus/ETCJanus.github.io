The Session

TouchDesigner was a wonderful tool to get to know, especially because I am relatively known with the node editor in Blender, this way of programming came intuitive. Of course experimenting with the MIDI-panel and seeing how the robot reacted was a good exercise. Starting off with base animations to express main feelings or emotions while also being able to later add layers (e.g. shaking) to make some pretty expressive animations with just a few simple mathematical formulas. The lecturer also was very enthusiastic which was infectious, making you want to be excited as well.

Our group initially had some issues with getting the robot to move but apparently we did not make one connection, this issue was easily solved with a bit of help. Afterwards we were up and running and did the assignments relatively quickly, we had a lot of fun with it and created some expressive animations in my opinion. It was a shame we did not have a lot of time during the lecture to keep experimenting with the robot. Nevertheless, here are some images and videos showcasing the practical.

Structured Observation

What the tool asked us to do: build base animations in TouchDesigner with the MIDI panel, and then layer extra movement on top of them.

What the arm produced: the shape of the claw makes it read as a head, so wherever it points it looks like it is looking. The simple curves already made it seem like it was calmly looking around (Video 1).

What we perceived: once we layered animations on top, like a shake, the arm suddenly read as mad or nervous. Layering animations really adds emotion, and interacting with it live (Video 2) made it feel surprisingly alive.

What this made visible: the emotion is not in what the arm does but in how it does it. The function (grab a bottle) and the feeling (nervous, calm) are separate layers you can design independently. That was not obvious to me before this session.

Reflection

Directly reusing the arm setup was never realistic for our case, Alan has no arm, only a driving base with light and sound. But two things from this session do apply. First, the idea that motion quality is its own design layer came back later in our group tool: the Movement field on the PAT robot cards describes how the robot moves separate from what it does, the Lag card for example says "hesitant, disjointed, and out of sync". That way of describing behaviour comes from this session (see the in-depth assignment). Second, Knight and Simmons [1] showed you can get Laban-style expressive motion out of nothing more than position and rotation of a mobile robot, which is exactly what Alan has. Speed, how direct the path is, and pauses are the expressive vocabulary left when there is no body.

What does not transfer is the audience. The session's vocabulary is aimed at human readers, a shake reads as nervous to us, but there is no guarantee a dog reads it the same way. For our case every expressive choice would need to be checked against how dogs actually respond to robot movement [2], not against what humans feel watching it.

References

[1] Knight, H., & Simmons, R. (2014). Expressive motion with x, y and theta: Laban Effort Features for mobile robots. In Proceedings of the 23rd IEEE International Symposium on Robot and Human Interactive Communication (RO-MAN 2014) (pp. 267-273). IEEE.

[2] Zamansky, A., Bleuer-Elsner, S., Masson, S., Amir, S., Magen, O., & Van der Linden, D. (2018). Effects of anxiety on canine movement in dog-robot interactions. Animal Behavior and Cognition, 5(4), 380-387.
