Design Tool

Description

This concept was developed during the second Social Robot Design session with Group 1. The focus was scenario-based design and a specific tool for Human-Robot Interaction (HRI). Instead of writing standard text scenarios, I proposed integrating Virtual Reality (VR) to modernize the improvisation theatre approach. During the execution, my role involved setting up the VR environment and acting as the technical operator and observer. I tracked the interactions from an outside perspective to identify design flaws.

Design Tool: Embodied Perspective Theatre (EPT) is a VR-assisted scenario-based design tool. Improvisation theatre is an established simulation tool for HRI design. Simulating an animal's experience introduces physical limitations because human actors possess human height and proportions. EPT solves this by placing actors in a VR environment where scale, eye-level, and physical presence are altered. One user embodies a small pet avatar, while another embodies the robot or human.

VR is primarily visual and lacks full tactile feedback. It does serve a critical function by forcing the designer into the exact viewpoint and mindset of the end-user. This mirrors the approach of industrial designer Patricia Moore, who wore restrictive bindings and altered glasses for years to experience product design through the perspective of an 80-year-old. EPT does this digitally. By adopting the pet's scale, the design team can identify spatial issues, threatening approach speeds, and blocked escape routes that standard written scenarios miss.

Results: Tool in Action

We used the tool to generate two distinct scenarios to map the boundaries of our pet companion robot. The VR perspective shifted the focus from what the robot does to how its actions are perceived. A slow approach for a human can feel like a rapid, trapping maneuver to a small animal.

This ties directly into my current thesis work, which uses VR to make training environments for first-responders more realistic. In both cases, the value of the tool lies in immersion. A robot does not need to be fully functional to test its social impact. Low-fidelity VR prototypes and acted scenarios are sufficient to expose critical constraints regarding movement speed, safety, and spatial awareness early in the design process.

Two Scenarios

Dark scenario

Misread Anxiety

The owner leaves, and the anxious dog paces near the front door. The robot's sensors detect the movement and incorrectly classify it as an invitation to play. The robot approaches, playing a sound and moving a toy module. From the dog's perspective, a large object is suddenly blocking its only exit. The dog backs away, which the robot misinterprets as a continuation of play, tracking it further. The dog eventually freezes by the couch in distress. The robot logs the lack of movement as a resting state and notifies the owner that the dog is calm.

Light scenario

Optional Engagement

The owner leaves for work, leaving the dog unsettled. Instead of initiating an interaction, the robot remains stationary at its charging base and uses a soft light to indicate it is active. The dog paces and eventually investigates a toy. The robot responds by nudging the toy slightly and stopping. The dog initiates play voluntarily. When the owner checks the companion app, the robot reports the duration of the physical interaction rather than making an unverified claim about the dog's emotional state.
