Project Collaborators

Design Tool

Description

This concept was created during an early morning meeting at the university library during a brainstorm where the focus was on designing a tool for scenario-based design. While we already had our focus on designing for pets, we thought it would be especially important for this userbase to immerse yourself in their perspective. During these early morning hours an idea landed on me: during my Research Topics I am doing research on using VR for making the training for first-responders more realistic. We could use this wonderful technology here too! Use VR to put yourself in the shoes of your user to get a better understanding of their experience.

Design Tool: Embodied Perspective Theatre (EPT) is a VR-assisted scenario-based design tool. Improvisation theatre is an established simulation tool for HRI design [5]. Usually it is hard to simulate the experience of an animal because human actors are limited to their humanly heights and proportions. EPT solves this by placing actors or designers directly in a VR environment where scale, sight and proportions are altered. One user can embody a pet while another can be the robot. Although VR is mostly visual and lacks actual tactile feedback, it does help force a designer to look through the exact viewpoint and mindset of the end-user.

HRI Grounding

The structure we used comes from scenario-based design [6]. A scenario needs an actor, a goal, a context and a complication, and writing those out forces you to make decisions you would otherwise skip. On top of that we used the rosy and dark framing: for each idea we wrote a version where everything goes right and one where it goes wrong. The dark scenarios are basically small science fiction prototypes [1], you use a made up story to find real problems before they exist. What makes EPT HRI specific instead of a generic writing exercise is that the robot in the scenario is only allowed to do things a real robot could sense and do, no magic understanding of the dog.

Results: Tool in Action

We used the tool to generate two distinct scenarios to map the boundaries of our pet companion robot. The VR perspective shifted the focus from what the robot does to how its actions are perceived. A slow approach for a human can feel like a rapid, trapping maneuver to a small animal. The concrete decision that came out of this for us: the robot should not approach an unsettled dog on its own, it waits and lets the dog make the first move. You can see this back in the light scenario below.

This ties directly into my current thesis work, which uses VR to make training environments for first-responders more realistic. In both cases, the value of the tool lies in immersion. A robot does not need to be fully functional to test its social impact. Low-fidelity VR prototypes and acted scenarios are sufficient to expose critical constraints regarding movement speed, safety, and spatial awareness early in the design process.

Two Scenarios

Dark scenario

Misread Anxiety

The owner leaves, and the anxious dog paces near the front door. The robot's sensors detect the movement and incorrectly classify it as an invitation to play. The robot approaches, playing a sound and moving a toy module. From the dog's perspective, a large object is suddenly blocking its only exit. The dog backs away, which the robot misinterprets as a continuation of play, tracking it further. The dog eventually freezes by the couch in distress. The robot logs the lack of movement as a resting state and notifies the owner that the dog is calm.

Light scenario

Optional Engagement

The owner leaves for work, leaving the dog unsettled. Instead of initiating an interaction, the robot remains stationary at its charging base and uses a soft light to indicate it is active. The dog paces and eventually investigates a toy. The robot responds by nudging the toy slightly and stopping. The dog initiates play voluntarily. When the owner checks the companion app, the robot reports the duration of the physical interaction rather than making an unverified claim about the dog's emotional state.

Reflective Questions

Sci-fi prototyping

This is a form of prototyping where we try to put a project or technology in the daily life of the future. Placing it in this context allows designers to identify pain points, unforeseen consequences, ethical issues and more [1]. Johnson also outlines a framework for sci-fi prototyping which involves picking a technology and the world around it, identify when it would change the world, consider ramifications, identify human inflection points and finally you reflect and adjust [1]. Design tools like these (e.g. future workshop, brainstorming/dumping, etc..) often rely on the creativity of the participators, which could results in amazingly unsurprising results. However, having the wrong group or focussing on the wrong aspects could give clouded results. Our current design tool also utilizes this aspect of placing yourself into the perspective of the user, however our design tool is based on the current tense. Focussing on how our pet robot would be used in the future where it is a staple in everybody’s house would mean the robot would need more autonomy than it currently has. Pets are a notoriously hard to design for, there are many methodological challenges in the use of robots in research because animals are so different from each other [2].

21st century robot project

Originally from Brian Johnson from the 21st Century Robot Project which is an open-source effort to democratize robotics. Instead only for industrial use, the project wants everyone to be able to design, program, and 3D-print their own robots [3]. For our project where we have the Alan robot which is also highly customizable, it can be very helpful to make it easier for others to also create their own props (e.g. treat dispenser, ball thrower) and attach it without issues, creating a toolkit which is more collaborative.

Rosy consumer stories

As used in the course, rosy consumer stories are great to find in which ways your current product can outshine or fail. What are the edge cases that make it special and thrive and what are the worst-case scenarios that can happen. Although these are nice ways to pitch your idea or get a greater understanding, it often leaves out the chaos of the real production and environment the product could land on [4]. For our design tool, it already helped us find the previously mentioned issue of the difference in pets, catching this issue early prevented our group of a lot of headaches later.

Importance of storytelling for HRI specifically

Everything in human interaction is about the social aspect and the expectations that go along with it, we learned this in Creative Technology and also now during the master Interaction Technology. Writing stories helps us map out these interactions and understand them on a deeper level. It also forces us, as humans, to put more effort into the expected interaction before even writing code. This helps us find issues but think of the interaction flow beforehand. We used this idea for our toolkit a lot, acting out the interaction between the robot and the animal is essentially creating a story in real time, although it is messy, it can lead to honest and new insights.

Evidence-based work (health) vs robot stories and perceived competence

It is very important, as we also learn during our course Human Robot Communication, that there is not a mismatch between the expected capabilities of the robot and what it can manage to do. If a robot’s story and perceived competence signal high intelligence, users will expect more of a robot, if this expectation is not met it introduces frustration and other issues. For our toolkit, it is important that we do not overpromise on the capabilities and that the form om the robot follows the function it can provide. Do not include a face and eyes if it is not meant to communicate.

Evaluation

Did the tool give a better result? I think yes. The insight about approach speed came directly from acting the scenario out, we would not have found that on a whiteboard. It changed how we thought about the robot approaching the dog in everything that came after.

Was the tool itself good? Mostly. Writing scenarios is fast and cheap and the rosy/dark split forces you to take failure seriously. The weak point is that everything depends on how well you play the dog, a human acting scared is still guessing, especially since animals differ so much from each other [2]. That is exactly the gap the PAT cards from the in-depth assignment try to close, by putting the dog behaviour on cards instead of leaving it fully to improvisation.
