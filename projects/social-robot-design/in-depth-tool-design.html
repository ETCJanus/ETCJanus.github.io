In-Depth Tool Design

Group assignment: PAT, the Pet Anxiety Toolkit, from concept to tested tool with the Alan robot.

Back to course page

Overview

Group work with Bianca Filip, Emilia Pavel, Gijs Vis and Maurits Dijkman. The task division for the toolkit: I did the card concept and the card decks plus the video script, Gijs built the props, Maurits wrote the test plan, Emilia made the observation sheets and Bianca made the poster. This page is my own write-up of the work. The tool is built for our case and grew out of the scenario design session.

Motivated Direction Choice

We chose the scenario design direction: interactive testing with the actual tech. The motivation comes straight from our case. The biggest problem with designing a robot for an anxious dog is that you cannot safely test ideas on a real anxious dog, an unfinished robot can cause exactly the stress it is supposed to reduce. The scenario design session had already shown us that acting an encounter out exposes interaction problems fast and cheap. What that course tool did not give us was structure and repeatability, improvisation depends heavily on who is acting and what they come up with on the spot [1]. PAT is our attempt to fix that: a scenario card sets the scene and goal, a robot card sets how the robot behaves or misbehaves, a dog card sets the dog's personality, and then you play the combination out with the real hardware.

Conceptualisation

We sketched the toolkit before building anything. My card notes from 8 June (Figures 1 to 3) set up the three categories, Scenario, Dog and Bot, with the first list of cards per category and the protocol: draw one card from each pile and play the combination out. The card fields follow the scenario elements from scenario-based design, an actor, a goal, a context and a complication [2]. The same day the physical side was sketched (Figures 4 to 7): a toolbox that holds the decks and props, a treat dispenser with swappable dispensing modules, a catapult style ball thrower, and removable side panels for Alan with different mounting options.

Physicalisation

From the sketches we went to building. The three decks were printed with a fixed structure per category: scenario cards have a Setting, Goal and Prop, robot cards a Movement and Interaction style, and dog cards a Main Action and an Edge-Case (Figure 8). Alan got a cardboard shell in segments with a 3D printed mount for the treat dispenser (Figure 9). Everything together forms the kit: the decks, the props, the panels and the manual in one box (Figures 10 and 11).

Test Plan and Method

We executed our test plan at the DesignLab on June 30. The test was formative, aiming to identify usability issues and interaction problems rather than gathering statistically representative data. The methodology required four roles: a dog player, a robot controller, a facilitator, and an observer.

During the session, Maurits acted as the dog, Emilia controlled the Alan robot, Ewoud facilitated the session, and Bianca recorded the observations. A strict safety protocol was established where the run would be paused if the individual acting as the dog signaled stress.

Deviations from the plan. Due to practical constraints, we deviated slightly from the initial plan: we omitted the participant questionnaire, and the testers were the same individuals who designed the toolkit.

Observations: Tool in Action

During the testing session, we logged three specific card combinations and filmed additional runs. The test runs revealed several concrete interaction failures:

Getting Familiar × Pushy × Scared: The robot continued to advance despite the dog not initiating approach. The robot encroached into a close proximity zone of less than 45 centimeters. The dog player was forced to retreat, and the run was paused due to the safety rule.

Routine Feeding × Too Many Treats × Optimal: The timing of the treat dispenser failed. Treats continued to dispense after the desired behavior stopped, which broke the necessary connection between the animal's action and the reward.

Navigation × Lag × Alpha: Delayed reactions from the robot control caused a near collision. Furthermore, the robot failed to yield right-of-way to an assertive dog that was blocking its path.

Filmed Runs: While filming additional scenarios, we observed physical hardware limitations. During a feeding run, the treat dispenser spout was too short, causing the food to miss the bowl. Additionally, the cardboard side panels I constructed were broken during a run simulating a rowdy dog.

Resulting Redesign

During testing, we found a few different aspects that were not working great. Although very obvious afterward, we did not include a manual for how our tool works and should be used, which caused confusion during setup. Therefore, we created one to improve upon our design, walking users through setup, execution, and observation without needing our external explanation. This manual can be seen in Figure 17.

We also discovered that the first iteration of the scenario cards was not as clear as we would have liked. Therefore, we expanded the selection of cards so that each of the three steps consists of five cards. We also color-coded the decks by category (yellow for scenarios, teal for the robot, and pink for the dog) and improved the clarity of the text. The improvements can be seen in Figure 16.

Another minor issue we found during the recorded feeding test was that the treat dispenser drops the treats too close to the body of Alan. Therefore, we created an iteration that has an extended chute to drop the treats further away from the body, ensuring it reaches the bowl.

During the rowdy dog testing scenario, we also found that the cardboard shell for Alan would not be strong enough when exposed to an actual dog. Although this did not surprise us, we did design a harder plastic shell with better mounting options than hot glue, although we were not able to actually manufacture it due to time and material restraints.

The final and most crucial issue we found was the current control method. The Alan robot is currently controlled through a Wii Nunchuk controller. This method of moving the robot, although effective, requires some practice. As we want the toolkit to focus on the behavior and interactions of the robot, and not on how to actually control said robot, this clumsy controller contaminates the test. I believe that for a further iteration, it would be important to improve this, either by having a different controller or by having software support the movement of the robot. This would ensure someone who has never controlled Alan can quickly start working with the toolkit, rather than practicing with the controls.

Evaluation and Reflection

Did the design get better? Yes, but in the form of named problems rather than finished solutions. Before testing, "the robot approaches the dog" was one line in a scenario. The tests turned that into concrete failure points for Alan: approach distance (the run got paused at under half a meter), reward timing (treats arriving after the behaviour break the connection), yielding when the path is blocked, and the controls themselves. Those are the things the next iteration has to solve, and knowing them this precisely is the improvement. Watching an anxious dog's movement being acted out also matches what Zamansky et al. describe for real dogs, the signal is in retreat, freezing and distance [3], and the idea that movement quality is its own design layer comes from the expressiveness session.

Is PAT good as a tool? The card structure is constraining in the good way, you cannot play a vague scenario, the cards force concrete behaviour. Usability outside our team was the weak point, people found starting confusing, which is why the manual and the colour coded decks exist. It generalises: swap the dog deck for a cat deck, or honestly for a human deck, and the protocol still works. I would recommend it, with the note that the robot controller is currently the bottleneck, driving Alan smoothly is hard, and a clumsy controller contaminates the test.

Personal reflection. It was interesting to see during the course how to design social robots and all the knowledge that comes along with it. It is not just a simple NAO robot every time, and it is difficult to grasp all the communication you are trying to give off. Building this toolkit was more difficult than I expected, but it was quite a fun challenge, although a bit confusing at times. Pets are a difficult user base to design for. I thought it would be easy at first, but they are almost more of a hassle than humans, ethically and physically. Alan was fun to use but hard to master.
