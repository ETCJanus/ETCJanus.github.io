Assignment 2.1 Completed

Design a Building Block: Screw Shapes

Challenge: Design a block that can be used for tinkering and quick prototyping.

I went through around ten different design concepts, trying out various ways to connect pieces. I experimented with flexible connections, rigid ones, permanent attachments, and temporary ones. I ended up combining ideas from my 9th and 10th sketches, and settled on a screw based design. I chose screws because they're really good for quick iteration. You can swap different shapes and sizes in and out really fast, but they're still strong enough to hold things together properly. Unlike permanent connections, you can quickly take them apart and rebuild, which is what makes them good for tinkering. I also used AI to generate some 3D renderings to visualize how the design would actually look and work.

Final Design: The screw shapes help iterate quickly.
Process: Exploring different shapes and connection types.
More Ideas: Brainstorming alternative attachments like magnets, velcro, and cardboard.
AI Rendering: Using AI to visualize material textures.
Assignment 2.2 Completed

Design a building block - fabricate

To bring my "screw shapes" concept into the physical world, I created a lo-fi prototype. I started by cutting basic shapes (cubes and blocks) out of wood. I then drilled holes into specific faces and attached standard screws using a glue gun.

The Tinkering Process: During assembly, I quickly realized that the spacing and depth of the screws were crucial for stability. I spent a good amount of time just playing around with different hole placements, testing how the pieces slotted together before committing to the final glue-up. This messy, hands-on iteration helped me discover the physical constraints of the material, something that looked perfect in my digital sketches didn't always friction-fit well in reality.

Finally, to add an extra layer of "tinkerability," I finished the blocks with a coat of black chalk paint. This allows the user to draw, write, or erase labels on the blocks themselves, rapidly changing their meaning or function on the fly during a brainstorming session.

Assignment 3.1 Completed

Properties of Tinkering Materials

Based on our discussions during the course, we evaluated several existing tinkering sets to identify which material properties score highest and lowest across different affordances.

Material Self-guiding Preciousness Skills-needed Interoperable Novelty Threshold / Ceiling
Totem Maker Kit Pretty self-explanatory, but some mistakes were easily made. Made of metal, feeling precious/expensive. Not cheap, but offers abundant parts. Barely any; just requires understanding how to connect stuff. (++) Its strongest suit. It allows you to connect easily with other tinkering tools. Not highly novel (mostly connector pieces), but the included saw is a nice touch. Low threshold to step in, but the ceiling isn't very advanced. Got boring quickly.
Minecraft Maker Kit Confusing. Felt like it was missing parts; could not do much with it. Materials felt expensive and fragile—not made to be messed around with heavily. Required prior understanding of coding and technology to use effectively. Could be linked to a Raspberry Pi, which is nice, but very limited beyond that. A bit outdated, though the core idea of adapting Minecraft for learning is novel. The worst part of the kit. Decently high threshold, and you feel played out after a day.
LittleBits Excellent self-guiding. Just playing around with it made us figure it out instantly. Materials are expensive and not easily swappable. Low score in this regard. Helps if you understand music, but you can successfully use it without prior skills. Potentially USB compatible, but unclear. Quite limited interoperability overall. Using sound as a primary tinkering medium is highly novel. Low threshold. We played outside the box, and the ceiling for sound production is quite high.

What struck me most was that LittleBits was the best one for actually tinkering around with, especially if you don't have any prior knowledge or expertise. It just made you curious and want to figure out what was possible. The Minecraft kit, on the other hand, felt more like a marketing product than something designed for real hands-on exploration.

Assignment 2.3 Completed

Design a building block - make it interoperable

From Structure to Sound

At the end of the last assignment, I had these functional interlocking wooden blocks with screws, but they didn't really have a clear purpose yet. During a feedback session in class, someone suggested that if I ran strings between them, they could work a bit like atoms in a chemistry model. Since my background is in music, hearing the word "strings" immediately sent me down a completely different path. I realized I could turn my tinkering blocks into a modular kit for building custom stringed instruments.

Designing the Interface

To actually connect strings to the blocks and put them under tension, I needed some kind of adapter. I made a 3D model of a threaded cylinder that can screw directly onto the exposed screws of my wooden blocks. Attached to this cylinder is a small mount designed to hold a standard guitar tuning peg. The idea is that you build a frame out of the blocks, add these adapters, and then use the guitar tuners to tighten the strings across the structure.

Lo-Fi Testing

Before committing to 3D printing and real metal strings, I did some lo-fi testing. I started by stringing rubber bands across the screws. This actually worked pretty well and showed that the block structures could handle basic tension while making a sound.

Then I bought some regular string and yarn to test out non-stretchy materials. In true tinkering fashion, I got completely sidetracked figuring out how to make the threads hold tension properly. I ended up falling down a rabbit hole and learning the basics of crochet and knitting just to try it out! While maybe not directly applicable to building a guitar, it was a great hands-on detour into understanding the material properties of strings.

The Piezo Upgrade

During class, I also got some really nice feedback that pushed the concept a bit further. A wise man suggested using those cheap little sound chips you find in musical greeting cards. It turns out those are basically simple piezo pickups that amplify vibration. So for my next iteration, the plan is to attach those to the wooden blocks. If I combine that with actual metal guitar strings, I can turn these acoustic blocks into customizable electric instruments.

Assignment 3.2 Completed

Properties of Tinkering Materials - extension

Continuing from the initial material evaluation in Assignment 3.1, I took a deeper look at three additional properties—Modularity, Interfaces, and Interoperability—for the same tinkering kits.

Modularity

Material Modularity
Totem Maker Kit It was mostly raw structural materials like beams, screws, and bolts. You can change a lot and link things together, but the parts aren't really self-contained, so you can't swap functional things out very quickly.
Minecraft Maker Kit This one was mostly just for visualizing builds IRL. It was quick, and each magnetic block did exactly what it had to do, but it's basically just blocks with magnets to visualize things.
LittleBits Really nice. Each block contains a separate, interesting part (like a sensor or a motor) making it super easy to change out components and build upon your previous iterations.

Interfaces

Material Interfaces
Totem Maker Kit The nuts and bolts interface gives it a lot of structural rigidity and makes it possible to create complex frames, but this definitely takes away from the speed of tinkering.
Minecraft Maker Kit Really quick to use. The magnets are nice to just quickly swap blocks around or try out different builds on the fly.
LittleBits The magnetic quick connection is really well and sleekly done. It's almost the pinnacle of how you want an interface to work for rapid iteration—you just snap them together and they instantly work.

Interoperability

Material Interoperability
Totem Maker Kit Because it's built upon the default hardware we use in the real world (standard screws, threads, and bolts), it's really nice to integrate with other building blocks, standard tools, or random materials you might have lying around.
Minecraft Maker Kit The blocks work well with each other, but the magnets are way too weak to really clamp something else in between them. You could maybe stick one on a fridge, but I wouldn't really call it interoperable.
LittleBits Out of the box, it mostly only works with what you have in the kit. There are specific modules out there (like AUX outputs for sound or bits for alligator clips) that let you break out of the ecosystem, but generally, the basic magnetic connection is pretty closed off to non-LittleBits materials.

Assignment 2.4 Completed

Design your building block - evaluate

To evaluate my electro-acoustic building blocks, I looked at the design through the lens of the material properties we discussed in class. Here is how the current iteration scores, along with some realizations for future improvements.

Self-guiding

Tensioning the strings is pretty intuitive because the design uses standard guitar tuning pegs, which most people are already familiar with. However, setting up the electrical extensions (like wiring the piezo pickups) is not as obvious and would likely require a bit more explanation or a manual to get right.

Preciousness

Very low. The materials (wood, standard screws, simple strings) are cheap and easy to source. I actually expect parts like the strings to snap or break over time, and the design embraces that. Because nothing feels expensive or fragile, users should not be afraid to be messy and push the limits of the materials.

Skills-needed

You only need a very basic understanding of how strings vibrate to get started. However, to get a genuinely good sound out of the blocks, the user is expected to learn (or already know) a bit about music theory, chords, and how to process digital audio signals.

Interoperable

The core connections rely on standard hardware screws, which is great because you can attach them to almost any scrap material. The tensioners can hold any type of string, yarn, or rubber band, and the AUX output for the audio is a universal standard. The custom 3D-printed adapters are pretty specific to this kit, but overall, it plays well with the outside world.

Novelty

I think an instrument creation toolkit is highly novel. It offers a completely different, hands-on way to learn about acoustics and music theory. Combining that physical experimentation with the electrical components makes the whole experience really interesting.

Threshold / Ceiling

The kit has a really nice range. The threshold is low: you can literally stretch a rubber band across two blocks and get your first "sound" in a matter of minutes. But the ceiling is high; building a properly tuned, electric instrument requires a lot of tweaking, learning, and iteration.

Modularity

It is a bit of a mixed bag. The main blocks and the piezo sensors are quite easy to swap out. The biggest bottleneck right now is the strings themselves; it is inherently difficult to quickly swap out a component that needs to be perfectly tensioned to function.

Interfaces

Right now, the physical frame relies entirely on screws to handle the tension of the strings, which works well. However, thinking through this evaluation made me realize a flaw: the electronic parts (like the battery pack or the piezo pickup) do not need to endure that same physical tension. In future iterations, I could use magnets or simple push-pins for the electronics. That would make the interface for the electrical components much faster for rapid prototyping.

Assignment 3.3 Completed

Properties of Tinkering Materials - wide walls

When talking about tinkering materials, wide walls is an important dimension. A material with wide walls supports many completely different types of projects, styles, and interests. For this assignment, I want to highlight a material I stumbled upon during this tinkering course: yarn and other textile crafts (think crochet, knitting, and weaving).

At its core, crochet and knitting are incredibly simple. It is basically pulling loops or simple knots through other loops. A beginner can learn the basic stitch within a few minutes to make a simple structure. The walls for this material are, however, incredibly wide. With that simple mechanism people are able to create:

  • Clothes, of course.
  • 3D stuffed animals (amigurumi).

But it has also appeared in unexpected places and projects.

For example, for over a century, mathematicians struggled to create a physical model of a hyperbolic plane. In 1997, mathematician Daina Taimina realized that crochet was the perfect way to represent non-Euclidean geometry. It has also been used to create other complex 3D objects like the Lorenz manifold.

Another story that fascinates me is from the Apollo Guidance Computer in the 1960s. Back then, software could not just be flashed onto a microchip. The code that helped astronauts had to be physically made by hand. NASA and MIT developed core rope memory, where they hired skilled women to literally weave computer software.

To me, the ultimate showcase of wide walls is yarn and the ways it has been used for centuries: a tinkering material that can be used for simple clothes and for bringing astronauts to the moon.

Tinkering Spaces - reflect back

The Desk

My desk is still a good spot, but looking back at it now it is a bit too simple. It is not really a place where I can break stuff or mess around freely. It is still just a bit too clean and precious. While it remains a nice place to tinker with code and digital projects, I learned that a real physical tinkering desk needs to be something else entirely, somewhere you do not mind making a huge mess.

The Piano

I still think music is a very valuable tinkering method and a great way to get into the flow. The piano especially is a perfect example of something with a really low threshold to start playing but a ceiling like almost no other tool. You can just sit down and press a key to get a result. And as you get better you can really get messy with it like when playing jazz, or you can prototype ideas really quickly through improv.

The Classroom

A classroom is obviously not really a place where you go to break physical stuff, but it acts as such an amazing facilitator. Having the presence of peers around you is great for bouncing off ideas and getting unstuck. But of course it lacks the physical tools and the freedom to fail that a place like the Design Lab has. The Design Lab basically takes the social part of the classroom and adds the actual toolbox. I have definitely tinkered way more in a place like that than I ever have in a normal classroom.

Overview on Design Methods

Brainstorming

Brainstorming is the messy part where ideas first come to life. It is the phase where a lot of failures can and should happen. You can do it collaboratively with peers or alone, but it essentially initiates the whole creation process.

Co-design

This method is important because you actively bring in different perspectives. Collaborating directly with users or people from other fields makes you tinker in ways you would never have thought of on your own.

Critical Design

Instead of designing a product to solve a specific problem, critical design is about designing something to ask a question or provoke a debate. It is meant to challenge assumptions and make people think differently about how we use technology or interact with the world.

Research through Design

Learning by doing and breaking things like Lego is definitely a huge part of physical tinkering and material exploration. But Research through Design specifically means using the actual process of designing something to generate new academic knowledge. You build a prototype not just to have a finished product, but to use the prototype itself as a research instrument to answer a bigger question.

Rapid Prototyping

This is basically the physical part of brainstorming. It is about seeing what works and what breaks as fast as possible. You shouldn't be too fancy or detailed here, just keep it quick and dirty. The faster you can physically change things, the more issues you find early on, which ultimately leads to a much better end product.

Iterative Design

This is the broader cycle that heavily relies on rapid prototyping. You just keep changing your design, finding mistakes, learning from them, and making improvements. You have users test it, gather their feedback, and then start the loop all over again until it actually works the way it should.

Assignment 5.2 Completed

Group Assignment: Physical MVP, first iteration

For our MVP we really wanted to get out of the laptops and onto the table. We are building a tool that lets you quickly and iteratively tinker with website layouts using physical cards. This makes the whole design process way more collaborative, especially for people who have less coding knowledge.

The Physical Cards

We started by creating several sheets of cards and laminating them so they can handle being tossed around and tested. Each card has a specific ArUco code on it that represents a different website component or layout option.

Card Sheets
The initial card designs with ArUco codes before lamination.
Laminated Cards
The finished laminated cards ready for hands-on testing and iteration.

The Technical Setup

We created a custom app to act as the bridge between the physical and digital world. Right now, the app successfully scans the cards on the table. It checks the ArUco codes and is able to see exactly which card is which, where it is placed, and what its orientation is.

The app then successfully sends all this data over to the laptop. We haven't actually built the website generation part yet, so the laptop just receives the raw data and doesn't translate it into a visual website right now. But the core pipeline works! The physical interface is laminated and ready to be played with, and the digital recognition is doing exactly what it should. For our next iteration, we just need to write the script that takes that incoming data and turns it into the actual website layout.
