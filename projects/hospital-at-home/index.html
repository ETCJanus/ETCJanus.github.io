Overview

Hospital at Home programs let patients with chronic heart conditions monitor their vitals at home instead of making regular hospital visits. Erasmus MC Rotterdam provides patients with a medical toolkit containing a blood pressure monitor, oxygen meter, thermometer, and scale. While this reduces hospital burden, it also places new responsibilities on patients, many of whom are older adults with limited health literacy and varying sensory abilities.

Our challenge was to redesign this toolkit to better support patients in their home environment, reducing cognitive load and anxiety while maintaining clinical accuracy.

What We Found: The Real Problems

To understand the patient experience, we visited the E-health House at the University of Twente, where we could test the actual medical devices in a simulated home setting. Some of us performed the full measurement routine while others observed and documented issues. We also conducted an expert interview with the program coordinator at Erasmus MC.

What became clear was that most problems weren't about the devices themselves but about how patients interact with them at home:

Sensory Confusion

Each device has its own feedback language. The thermometer beeps sharply, the blood pressure monitor flashes numbers, the scale stays silent. For patients, these ambiguous signals often feel like error warnings rather than confirmation, which increases anxiety during measurements.

Cognitive Overload

Patients must remember the correct order, operate four different devices with different interfaces, manually write down or type in each reading, and interpret whether the values are concerning. Almost no one reads the lengthy paper manuals, especially when feeling unwell.

Poor Accessibility

Small displays with low contrast make it nearly impossible for users with limited vision to read results. Configuration steps (like entering your birthdate into the scale) happen at inappropriate moments and create unnecessary barriers.

From the expert interview, we learned that many "medical errors" in the data are actually user errors that happen before the device even records a value. Things like taking blood pressure right after walking upstairs, not sitting properly, or moving during measurement. The current toolkit provides no real time guidance to prevent these upstream problems.

Our Users

Based on our research and the expert interview, we created two personas representing typical Hospital at Home patients. Both are older adults with chronic heart conditions, but with different living situations and abilities.

Renate Felicia, 68 | Lives with husband | Part-time work

Kees de Groot, 72 | Lives alone | Lower digital literacy

We used these personas throughout the design process to check whether our solutions would work for both someone living alone with limited tech skills and someone in a busy household with sensory constraints. We also created an empathy map and detailed patient journey to understand not just what users do, but what they think and feel during the measurement routine.

Our Solution: Build-A-Care Cabinet

Instead of redesigning the medical devices themselves (which are certified and need to stay as-is), we designed a cabinet that acts as an organizer, guide, and communication hub. The concept transforms the loose collection of tools into a single, guided ritual that fits naturally into the home.

Visually, it looks like a small wooden radio or piece of furniture. When closed, it blends into the bedroom or living room. When opened, it reveals dedicated compartments for each device arranged top to bottom: blood pressure monitor on top, oxygen meter and thermometer in the middle, and a sliding drawer with the scale at the bottom.

How It Works

The cabinet uses a shared language of light and sound to guide users through each step:

1. Reminder

At measurement time, the cabinet emits a soft glow and gentle chime (not a harsh alarm). It adapts to time of day: brighter and clearer in the afternoon, softer and warmer at night so it doesn't disturb others.

2. Guided Sequence

Press the large green Start button. The top compartment (blood pressure) lights up. Complete that measurement, and the system plays a confirmation chime, sends the data automatically, and lights up the next device. This continues down to the scale at the bottom. You never have to wonder what to do next or which device is which.

3. Error Support

If something goes wrong (you moved during measurement, or the reading seems off), the light turns amber and a calm voice message suggests what to try. Instead of feeling panicked, you get actionable guidance.

4. Automatic Data Transfer

Every successful measurement is automatically transmitted to the hospital's monitoring system. No apps to open, no numbers to write down or type in. When all four measurements are done, a green status light confirms everything went through.

Form as Comfort

Matte wood, rounded edges, and textile speaker grille make it feel like furniture, not medical equipment. This reduces the constant visual reminder of illness.

The physical design also uses "poka-yoke" (mistake proofing). Each device has a shaped slot that only fits the correct tool, and pictographic instructions are printed inside the door so they're always visible when the cabinet is open.

Design Process

We followed an iterative design process based on the understand, explore, and define model. After the e-health house visit and expert interview, we used several design methods to translate insights into solutions:

Lotus Blossom Technique

To widen our solution space, we looked at inspiring products from other domains. From senior mobile phones we borrowed large, clear buttons. From AEDs we took guided sequences and status indicators. From continuous glucose monitors we adopted automatic data transmission. From Nespresso machines we used the idea of shaped slots for mistake proofing.

Journey Mapping

We mapped a full day in the life of Renate (one of our personas) using the redesigned toolkit. This helped us see how the multisensory cues, error handling, and ritual would work across different times of day and emotional states.

Design Principles

From our research, we established principles that guided all design decisions: low cognitive effort, patient empowerment, multimodal feedback, tangible interaction quality, and support for habit formation. Every feature had to serve at least one of these principles.

My Role

In our seven person multidisciplinary team, I took on multiple responsibilities. I created all the 3D visualizations and product renders using Blender, which was important for communicating the concept to both our team and the client. I also contributed to the interaction flow design, helping define how the light and sound cues would guide users through the measurement sequence.

Beyond the design work, I helped coordinate the team throughout the project, organizing feedback sessions and making sure we stayed aligned with our research insights and design principles as we moved from concept to final proposal.

Outcome & Reflection

We presented our concept to Erasmus MC at the end of the project. They were very happy with what we delivered, particularly how we reframed the problem from "fixing devices" to "supporting the ritual." The concept showed them a direction they hadn't considered before, focusing on the emotional and sensory experience rather than just technical functionality.

For me personally, this project reinforced how much user research matters. Going to the e-health house and actually using those devices revealed problems we never would have found by just reading the brief. The disconnect between what clinically "works" and what patients actually experience at home was eye opening. It also taught me the value of design methods like personas and journey mapping. They aren't just academic exercises, they genuinely help you stay focused on real user needs when you're deep in design decisions.

The biggest challenge was balancing the clinic's constraints (certified devices that can't change, reusable boxes, data security) with what patients actually needed (simplicity, reassurance, home appropriate aesthetic). Finding that balance by adding a guiding layer rather than replacing everything felt like the right approach for this context.
